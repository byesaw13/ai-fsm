## Structural fixes to your pricing model

The strongest long-term fix is to stop relying on category labels like “core,” “standard,” and “specialty” by themselves. They are useful internally, but they are not precise enough to prevent bad estimates. The book should instead be built on five layers: **dispatch minimum**, **book hours**, **scope assumptions**, **modifiers**, and **quote-only triggers**. Guidance from urlJobberhttps://www.getjobber.com and urlHousecall Prohttps://www.housecallpro.com is consistent on the fundamentals: build pricing from labor, materials, overhead, billable hours, and profit rather than guessing from hourly wage alone. citeturn26search0turn26search4turn26search8turn27search3turn27search19turn27search20

The most dangerous lines in your current book are the ones that are too broad. “Faucet replacement,” “crown molding install/repair,” “under-cabinet lighting installation,” “fence painting/staining,” “deck staining/sealing,” “shed assembly,” “storm window installation,” “garage organization,” “minor siding repairs,” and “screen porch repair” are not single products. They are families of work with wildly different labor times, access needs, materials, and risk. A durable price book either splits those items into narrower codes or makes them quote-only after a quick scope review. The reason is visible in published product pricing: distinct product types already move the market materially on faucets, toilets, blinds, TVs, and deck work. citeturn29search1turn29search5turn29search2turn31search3turn30search0turn31search0turn31search4

The other structural fix is visit logic. Drywall, paint touchups, texture matching, and some exterior seal/stain work often have **dry time**, not just labor time. If you treat them as one straight labor block, you undercount travel, masking, setup, cleanup, and return-trip overhead. The revised book below assumes that “book hours” mean **billable field hours**, not cure time, and it adds a separate return-visit minimum when the job really needs two trips. That is a much more durable system than quietly eating the second trip. citeturn23view0turn23view4

The final fix is bundle rules. Local franchised players are not winning every small job because they are faster with a screwdriver; they are winning because package pricing makes punch lists easy to buy. Your book should do the same. Once a customer crosses three small tasks, two rooms, or roughly four book hours, individual line-item stacking should usually stop and half-day, full-day, or fixed-estimate pricing should take over. That is how you protect margin and stay competitive without playing the race-to-the-bottom game. citeturn17view2turn17view3turn17view4turn17view6

## Bundle rules and annual updates

The most profitable way to use this book is with bundles. A sound same-visit rule is: first stand-alone task at full retail, second small add-on in the same room or same discipline at about 80% of stand-alone price, and third-plus low-complexity add-ons at about 65% to 75% of stand-alone price. That works because the market already prices small jobs around mobilization minimums; once that first trip is paid for, you can become very competitive while still protecting margin. Local half-day and full-day package pricing from the companies above proves that customers are already conditioned to buy blocks of time when the to-do list is long enough. citeturn17view2turn17view3turn17view4turn17view6turn36search14

A good default bundle structure is straightforward. A **bath refresh** with a faucet, showerhead, toilet fill valve, and one accessory install should usually be estimated from **2.5 to 3.5 book hours** instead of stacking four separate minimums.
